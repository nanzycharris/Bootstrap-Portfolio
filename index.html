Similar to the fictional location of a short story written by a Latin-American author representative of the magical realism, Nancy Cantú Morúa was born in a dusty, barren, and uncharismatic town on the northern border of Mexico, on the south side of the Rio Grande.

The first of four children that the marriage of Lupita Morua, a dressmaker, and Mercedes Cantu, a restaurant waiter, produced, Nancy grew up Catholic, watching overdramatic actors and simplistic plots from telenovelas with her mother, and reading with her father about history and science from the various encyclopedias proudly displayed in their living room bookcase.

Raised Catholic, Nancy took her first communion in her elementary school years. She didn’t have a quinceañera, instead, she asked for a family trip. In lieu of a grand celebration, her parents packed the family in their used white Ford Aerostar, and decided to invite her maternal grandmother, step-grandfather, and as many uncles and aunts as they could fit in the vehicle, and drove for what seemed an eternity but was only a couple of hours to the lackluster beach of Matamoros on her 15th birthday.

There was no cake, or mariachis to sing Las Mañanitas to her, no one even remembered to say “Feliz cumpleaños” to her. Paradoxically, she’s thankful for those memories and she credits them with giving her the strength and determination to graduate college.

Nancy worked and saved for two years after high school so that she could move to Monterrey at the age of 19 to attend college at the Universidad Autonoma de Nuevo Leon. Holding at times two jobs and a full load of credits every semester and summer, our petite light skin, brown eyed girl managed to obtain her B.A. in Communications with a 3.64 GPA.

After college, Nancy worked as a writer and news reporter for several printed, electronic, and digital media outlets in her hometown of Reynosa and Monterrey. She found moderate success in her field and never again went to bed with hunger pains.

In 2009, our subject changed trajectories and came to live in the United States after marrying James Harris, from Garland, Texas. Nancy stopped being Cantú Morúa to become Cantu Harris, and in 2012 she gave birth to a daughter they named Teresa after Mother Teresa.

In recent years, Nancy C. Harris has enjoyed overcoming a severe depression, and a bad marriage, both probably related. She currently works as a Spanish teacher for children 2-5 years old at a private Christian school in south Dallas, and loves taking technology classes and reading books in English and Spanish with her daughter Teresa at bedtime.
